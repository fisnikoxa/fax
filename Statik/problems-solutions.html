Statics - Force Systems and Resultants

üìö Key Concepts and Formulas

1. Vector Representation of Forces

Why: Forces have both magnitude and direction, so we use vectors

Formula: \(\mathbf{F} = F_x\mathbf{i} + F_y\mathbf{j} + F_z\mathbf{k}\)

When to use: When describing any force in 2D or 3D space

Unit vector along a direction: \(\hat{u} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{|\mathbf{r}|}\) (divides vector by its magnitude)

2. Resultant Force (Sum of Forces)

Why: Multiple forces can be replaced by a single equivalent force

Formula: \(\mathbf{R} = \sum \mathbf{F}_i = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 + \mathbf{F}_3 + ...\)

In components: \(R_x = \sum F_{ix}\), \(R_y = \sum F_{iy}\), \(R_z = \sum F_{iz}\)

Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2 + R_z^2}\)

3. Moment of a Force (Tendency to Rotate)

Why: Forces cause rotation about points - this is the moment

2D Formula: \(M_O = F \times d_{\perp}\) (force √ó perpendicular distance)

3D Formula: \(\mathbf{M}_O = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}\) (cross product)

When to use: Finding rotational effect of a force about a point

Sign convention: Counterclockwise = positive, Clockwise = negative (in 2D)

4. Cross Product (for 3D Moments)

Why: Gives a vector perpendicular to both input vectors (represents rotation axis)

\[\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix}\]

Expanded: \((A_yB_z - A_zB_y)\mathbf{i} - (A_xB_z - A_zB_x)\mathbf{j} + (A_xB_y - A_yB_x)\mathbf{k}\)

When to use: Calculating 3D moments, finding perpendicular vectors

5. Dot Product (Projection)

Why: Finds component of one vector along another direction

Formula: \(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\cos\theta\)

When to use: Finding force component along a line, checking perpendicularity

6. Equivalent Force-Couple System

Why: Any force system can be replaced by a force + moment at a point

Steps:

1. Calculate resultant: \(\mathbf{R} = \sum \mathbf{F}_i\)

2. Calculate moment about point O: \(\mathbf{M}_O = \sum (\mathbf{r}_i \times \mathbf{F}_i)\)

When to use: Simplifying complex force systems, moving forces to different points

7. Line of Action

Why: A resultant force acts through a specific line in space

Finding distance: \(d = \frac{M_O}{R}\) (moment divided by force)

When to use: Locating where the resultant force acts

8. Common Trigonometry (for force components)

Adjacent/Horizontal component: \(F_x = F\cos\theta\)

Opposite/Vertical component: \(F_y = F\sin\theta\)

3-4-5 Triangle: If sides are 3:4:5 ratio, it's a right triangle

Pythagorean theorem: \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\)

Solution

üìñ Approach: When multiple forces act at a point, we find the resultant using vector addition.

Formula to use: For angle Œ∏ from horizontal ‚Üí \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(F_y = F\sin\theta\)

Step 1: Resolve each force into x and y components

Problem 2/81

Solution

üìñ Approach: Equivalent force-couple system at O

Key concept: Any system of forces can be replaced by a single resultant force R plus a couple M_O at a chosen point

Formulas:

1. Resultant force: \(R = \sum F_i\) (algebraic sum since all forces are horizontal)

2. Moment at O: \(M_O = \sum (r_i \times F_i)\) plus any applied couples

3. Line of action: \(d = M_O / R\) (perpendicular distance from O)

Step 1: Calculate the resultant force

Why: Forces on the same line add algebraically (‚Üí positive, ‚Üê negative)

From diagram: 4 kN (‚Üí), 10 kN (‚Üí), 6 kN (‚Üê)

\[R = 4 + 10 - 6 = 8 \text{ kN (to the right)}\]
Step 2: Calculate moment about point O

Formula: \(M_O = \sum M_i = M_{\text{applied}} + \sum (F_i \times d_i)\)

Sign convention: Counterclockwise (CCW) = positive, Clockwise (CW) = negative

Why this works: Each force creates rotation about O. The moment arm is the perpendicular distance.

Applied couple: 15 kN¬∑m (CCW) ‚Üí +15

4 kN at 0.3 m above O: Causes CCW rotation ‚Üí +(4)(0.3) = +1.2

\[M_O = 15 + 1.2 + 5 - 6 = 15.2 \text{ kN¬∑m (CCW)}\]
Step 3: Find line of action of stand-alone resultant

Concept: A single force R can replace the force-couple system if placed at the right location

Formula: \(d = \frac{M_O}{R}\) (moment = force √ó distance)

Why: The resultant must create the same moment about O

\[d = \frac{M_O}{R} = \frac{15.2}{8} = 1.9 \text{ m}\]

Since M_O is positive (CCW) and R points right (‚Üí), the force must act 1.9 m above O

Line equation: Horizontal line at y = 1.9 m

‚úì Answer:

Force-couple at O: R = 8 kN (‚Üí), M_O = 15.2 kN¬∑m (CCW)

Stand-alone resultant: R = 8 kN (‚Üí) acting at y = 1.9 m
